Firefighting Robot — Step-by-step assembly + orthogonal sketches

Assumptions: standard wooden popsicle stick = 114 × 10 × 2 mm. All measurements are in mm. This document gives a practical, budget-friendly prototype build sized to use popsicle sticks and low-cost components.

Quick specs (target)

  • Total height (approx): 310 mm (legs 120 + torso 150 + head 40).
  • Torso: 100 mm wide × 150 mm high × 30 mm deep.
  • Leg length: 120 mm each (from hip to wheel axle).
  • Wheel diameter: 60 mm. Wheelbase (center-to-center): 120 mm.
  • Arm reach (shoulder to nozzle): 120 mm.
  • Reservoir: small plastic bottle ~200 mL (Ø 60 mm × 80 mm high).

Parts (short)

  • Arduino Nano or Uno, motor driver (L298N), MOSFET (IRLZ44 or similar) for pump
  • 2 × DC gear motors (with wheels) — wheel Ø 60 mm
  • 2 × small caster wheels (Ø 25–30 mm) for balance
  • 2 × micro servos (SG90) for pan/tilt of nozzle
  • 3 × flame sensors, 1 × HC-SR04 ultrasonic
  • 1 × 3–6V small submersible pump (or inline 5V pump), tubing, nozzle
  • Battery pack (7.4V or appropriate for motors) + 5V UBEC/regulator for servos/Arduino
  • Popsicle sticks, hot glue, small plywood motor plates, small screws, zip ties

Step-by-step assembly

  1. Prepare popsicle-stick panels — glue sticks side-by-side to make flat panels.
    • Torso panel: 10 sticks wide → 100 mm width, glue 3 layers cross-grain for thickness ≈ 6 mm.
    • Leg panels: make narrow panels 3 sticks (30 mm) wide; glue 4–5 sticks long to reach 120 mm.
  2. Cut motor mount plates — small plywood rectangles 60 × 40 × 3 mm for gearmotor mounting. Drill holes to secure motor with small screws.
  3. Build the torso box — use two large panels (front/back) 150 × 100 mm and side strips 150 × 30 mm.
    • Glue front and back panels to side strips to form a shallow box 100 × 150 × 30 mm. Inside add a small shelf (popsicle-stick strip) at 60 mm from bottom to hold motor driver and battery strap.
  4. Assemble legs — for each leg create a rectangular column 120 × 30 × 30 mm made by folding/gluing panels around a 30 mm square core. Reinforce corners with extra sticks.
    • At the bottom attach the plywood motor plate per leg so that the motor axle centers at the bottom center of the leg column.
  5. Mount motors & wheels — fix gearmotors to motor plates; attach wheels (Ø60 mm). Check wheel axis height: with wheel fitted the axle should be ~8 mm below the leg bottom so wheel clears the frame.
  6. Attach caster(s) — add 2 small swivel casters under the front or center (choose front of robot as small bump-prevention). Place casters about 40 mm forward of wheel axle to prevent tipping.
  7. Mount torso to legs — glue or screw each leg to the inside bottom edge of the torso box. Leg centers spaced 120 mm apart (center-to-center). Ensure the water tank can fit between legs inside the torso base area.
  8. Prepare arms — build two arm assemblies: shoulder link (30 × 20 × 10 mm) and forearm (~120 × 18 mm long). Cut small wooden brackets for servo mounts.
    • Shoulder servo (pan) mounts to the top-front corner of torso; attach arm to servo horn.
    • Wrist servo (tilt) mounts near arm end with a short bracket; nozzle attaches to wrist horn.
  9. Install water reservoir & pump — place small bottle (Ø ~60 mm) low and centered in torso. Secure with zip ties or glue. Place pump either inside the reservoir (submersible) or inline between reservoir and arm tubing. Make sure pump outlet faces up into tubing.
    • Route tubing from pump up through torso, into shoulder, through arm channel, to nozzle.
  10. Mount electronics — attach Arduino Nano to the torso inner back wall, motor driver on shelf below Arduino, MOSFET & pump wiring nearby. Keep servos anchored to the 5V regulator. Keep battery strapped to lower shelf next to reservoir to keep COG low.
  11. Wire motors & servos — connect motors to motor driver, motor driver to battery; Arduino digital pins to driver inputs (with PWM). Connect servos to 5V regulated supply; route servo signal wires to Arduino pins. Connect flame sensors to analog inputs and HC-SR04 to digital pins.
  12. Mount sensors — glue or screw flame sensors across chest (left/center/right) at about shoulder height 200 mm from ground. Mount ultrasonic sensor centered above the three sensors near top of torso (about 220 mm high).
  13. Test electronics on bench — before final sealing: power up Arduino and sensors, verify motor direction, servo movement, flame sensor readings (log analog values), ultrasonic distance readings, pump switching via MOSFET (do dry run without water first or with pump out of reservoir).
  14. Final assembly & cable management — glue low-stress joints, use zip ties to route tubing & wires inside leg and torso channels; add small splash guard plate at front of electronics to minimize water exposure.
  15. Calibration & baseline tests — calibrate flame sensor thresholds (record analog values at 1m, 50 cm, 20 cm from small candle), set PWM for slow approach speeds, tune servo angles for aiming ±30°.
    • Set conservative spray distance (sprayRange) at 30–50 cm depending on pump/nozzle capability.
  16. Field test (controlled) — outdoors or with fire safety: test SEARCH → AIM → APPROACH → SPRAY cycles on a small candle. Keep a person with extinguisher nearby.

Notes & tips (final)

  • If you need more rigidity, add thin plywood braces inside the torso and at leg joints.
  • Keep electronics elevated above the reservoir top and use waterproof tape on seams near tubing.
  • If using a submersible pump, run the pump on a switched line with a MOSFET and a diode across the motor if required by the pump type.
  • Adjust the arm length if your pump flow is weak — shorter arms mean better spray force at close range.
